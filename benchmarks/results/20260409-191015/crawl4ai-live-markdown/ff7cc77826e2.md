🌶️Shared some takes on [Lenny](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lennyrachitsky?trk=public_post-text)’s podcast that might upset the AI hype crowd. Not the safest takes, but it is what it is🤷🏻♀️ Here are a few: 1. AI products’ core differences, like non determinism and the agency control trade off, are largely ignored, and this leads to fundamental development mismatches. These properties directly shape system behavior, yet most AI products are still designed as if they behave like traditional software. 2. Reliability, not just capability, is the biggest blocker for enterprise AI adoption. While most conversations fixate on model performance or new features, reliability is what actually stops enterprises from shipping customer facing AI. 3. Start small and iteratively build AI products with high human control and low agency, forcing a problem first approach. This goes against the instinct to jump straight to autonomous systems in a hyped space. 4. Leaders need to get hands on again and be vulnerable, accepting that they might be the dumbest person in the room when it comes to AI. This challenges traditional leadership instincts and reliance on past intuition. 5. A FOMO driven culture, rather than an empowering one, can actively sabotage AI product development. Fear among subject matter experts blocks collaboration and slows real progress. 6. Obsessing over understanding specific workflows matters more than obsessing over the underlying AI technology itself. Deep process knowledge beats tech fascination every time. 7. Success in AI isn’t about being first to launch an agent, it’s about building the right flywheels for continuous improvement. Less glamorous, far more effective. 8. One click agents are often pure marketing and should be met with serious skepticism. Real deployments involve messy data, brittle infra, and long term effort. 9. Evals aren’t a cure all and need to be balanced with production monitoring. Focusing on just one creates a false dichotomy. 10. Persistence is the new mode, and pain is the new moat in AI product development. The difficulty itself becomes a competitive filter. If any of this resonates, these are some of the ideas we teach in our Maven course, in far more depth, shaped by what we’ve learned across 50+ real world implementations. I agree to the thoughts shared by [Aishwarya Naresh Reganti](https://www.linkedin.com/in/areganti?trk=public_post-text). Here is my take on this : 1. Design for non-determinism upfront → Build retry logic, fallback paths, and output validation into every AI feature from day one. Don't treat flaky outputs as bugs to fix later. 2. Reliability over capability → Before adding new model features, ask: "Can we guarantee this works 99% of the time?" Ship boring reliable AI before impressive unreliable AI. 3. Start with human-in-the-loop → Your first version should have a human approval step. Remove it only after you have data proving it's unnecessary. 4. FOMO culture kills collaboration -> This is a great point. Don't let "competitor X launched an agent" drive the roadmap. Everyone has a unique roadmap. 5. Evals aren't enough - > Passing your test suite doesn't mean it works in prod. Monitor real traffic, latency, error rates, token usage, user feedback, PII scrubbing performance. Build dashboards that show drift between eval performance and live performance. Kind of more harder problem to tackle. 6. Build for continuous improvement and Ship the feedback loop infrastructure early, not after launch.
