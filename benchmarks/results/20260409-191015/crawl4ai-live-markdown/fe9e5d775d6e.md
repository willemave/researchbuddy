#  AI Product Leadership Interviews Shift: UX Evaluation Returns
This title was summarized by AI from the post below.
Hackerdogs•15K followers
Something interesting is happening in AI product leadership interviews. Companies are quietly adding UX evaluations back into the hiring process. Not for design roles. For AI product strategy roles. I've been in rooms where the interview loop for a Head of AI Product includes a dedicated UX interview — conducted by a principal-level designer — specifically to assess whether the candidate understands how humans actually interact with intelligent systems. This would have been unthinkable two years ago. Back then, everyone wanted the person who could talk to the ML team and ship models. UX was a "nice to have" that happened downstream. So what changed? Companies shipped AI products that nobody wanted to use. They built impressive technology that users abandoned after a week. They launched copilots and assistants that technically worked but felt hostile. They watched competitors with "worse" models win because the experience was better. The hard truth the market is learning: the hardest problem in AI isn't the model. It's building products customers actually want to buy. And that requires someone who understands both the technology AND the human on the other side of it. The AI products I've seen succeed — across dozens of launches — weren't the most technically sophisticated. But they weren't chatbots. They were the ones where the team obsessed over the experience as much as the algorithm. If you're hiring for AI product leadership and your interview loop doesn't evaluate UX sensibility, you're about to repeat the most expensive mistake in enterprise AI. The pendulum is swinging. Pay attention.

I replaced myself in product discovery interviews. (Well… partially.) Over the last two weeks, I’ve been running AI-led product discovery interviews at Cynozure. Instead of booking back-to-back stakeholder calls, I: – Trained a model on transcripts from real past interviews – Defined the structure and sequencing – Told it when to probe and when to summarise – Asked participants to respond using voice, not just text Then I let it run. 🤖 What happened? First surprise: People loved the flexibility. They could do it in their own time, pause halfway through and reflect before answering. 👌 What worked really well As a product manager, I got a lot of usable insight. It was particularly strong for: • Understanding processes • Mapping data flows • Surfacing inconsistencies • Creating structured, comparable outputs Consistency was particularly powerful. As human interviewers, we try to follow a structure, but we never really do. AI does exactly what it’s told. Reviewing the transcripts was faster, cleaner, and surprisingly rich. Interestingly, people were more meandering with AI — sometimes in very helpful ways. And selfishly? No interview fatigue. If you’ve ever joined a new organisation and run 10+ discovery interviews in a week, you’ll know how cognitively intense it is to listen, process, and probe at the same time. This removed that bottleneck. 😬 Where it wasn’t perfect Some participants felt that it didn’t guide them enough, while others felt they were guided too much. Everyone wished it spoke back instead of just typing. It was good at summarising, but not amazing at turning insight into a meaningful backlog. And there were absolutely moments I wished it dug deeper. But honestly? That happens with human interviewers too - we all probe differently. 💁♀️ The important bit: the human touch I want to be clear - I’m not saying Product Manager interviews should be all replaced with AI. Human connection isn’t going anywhere. But you can be more strategic. For example: – Tier stakeholders: key ones in person, broader group via AI – Use AI for early discovery, then run deeper live sessions – Surface patterns and company understanding with AI before human workshops It’s augmentation, not replacement. ✍ My takeaway? AI-led discovery absolutely belongs in the product toolkit. However, it needs iteration, thoughtful design and experimentation. But when used intentionally, it scales insight without sacrificing quality. Would you trust AI to run part of your discovery process? Where would you draw the line?

Before changing anything, Canva tested their own interview questions against AI tools. The result? AI solved them. Correctly. In seconds. No follow-up prompts needed. So they scrapped the entire format and started over. The new interview looks very different. Instead of standard algorithm problems, candidates now face complex, ambiguous, product-realistic scenarios — the kind that can’t be solved with a single prompt. AI tools aren't banned. They're expected. Instead of: "Implement Conway’s Game of Life." Candidates might get something like: "Design a control system for managing aircraft takeoffs at a busy airport." What the pilot revealed was interesting. The candidates who struggled weren’t the ones who couldn’t code. They were the ones who: • couldn't tell when AI was wrong • didn't know how to steer it when it went off track The strongest candidates behaved differently. They: • asked clarifying questions before writing code • shaped the AI output instead of blindly accepting it • debugged confidently when things broke One internal concern came up: "Are we replacing rigorous CS fundamentals with vibe-coding sessions?" Canva’s answer: Depth and fundamentals still matter. They're just evaluated in a context that actually resembles the job. If your interview process was designed before AI tools became standard, you're probably not evaluating how candidates will actually work. You're evaluating performance in a simulation of a job that no longer exists. 🔗
